Data Introduction The "Police Department Incident Reports Historical 2003 to May 2018.csv" file offers comprehensive information about criminal incidents that happened in San Francisco between January 1, 2003, and May 15, 2018.The San Francisco Police Department (SFPD) collected, analyzed, and made the information publicly accessible through the San Francisco Open Data Portal. The dataset contains details on the incident's date, time, and place as well as a basic description of the incident type (such as theft or larceny, assault, burglary, etc.).It also contains additional precise information about each occurrence, such as the crime's description, if an arrest was made, and the victim's and suspect's racial and ethnic backgrounds. The size of the dataset is quite large, with over 1.5 million rows and 35 columns.

After the aforementioned findings and being motivated by the video, we started looking into the relationship between alcohol use and the growth of crime in these two places. Retail shops known as liquor stores are dedicated to the sale of alcoholic drinks such beer, wine, and spirits. These shops might also sell food, mixers, and other things that go well with alcoholic beverages. Several jurisdictions have stringent laws governing the location, business hours, and who is permitted to buy alcohol at liquor stores. In certain places, liquor stores are run and owned by the government, whereas in other places, they are run and owned by private businesses. Indeed, high levels of alcohol outlet density can contribute to various negative outcomes, including increased alcohol consumption, impaired driving, and violent crime. Pamela J.Trankenstein et al. in a survey have proven that a 10% increase in alcohol outlet access was associated with a 4.2% increase in violent crime exposure. Off-premise outlets such as these liquor stores were found to have a greater association with violent crime than on-premise outlets such as bars or pubs. Other results show that opening a new alcohol outlet increases both violent and property crimes, especially in low socioeconomic status neighborhoods[1].
